February 2015

Who teaches and why?

  • Our instructors are scientists in many career stages, from many fields
  • Most are not computer scientists or software engineers
  • "Conscious competence": still new enough to relate to beginners
  • Many follow learner -> helper -> instructor path

Motivating learners

  • Instructors explain how Software Carpentry skills help their own research
  • Demonstrating that instructors care about learners is key motivator
  • Helping less-experienced learners catch up means they don't feel lost
  • Learners attending a workshop as a lab group provide mutual support

Hands-On

  • Remembering content is aided by active (as opposed to passive) learning
  • Short exercises & challenges let learners 'try it out' immediately
  • Help is available so learners don't get stuck

Feedback

  • Instructors' live-coding involves making mistakes, shows it's OK
  • Learners provide instant feedback ("OK/not OK") via colored sticky notes
  • Sticky notes also used for short written feedback after a session
  • Being responsive to feedback imposes practical limits to the size of workshops

Pacing

  • Lessons are broken into digestible sizes
  • Time for breaks built in: sessions limited to 1.5 h
  • Each of 4 topics gets about one half-day

Helpers

  • Often former learners; workshop instructors help when not leading
  • Usually <10 learners per helps
  • Help learners with challenges, take notes on Etherpad
  • Provide feedback to instructors

    • Collaborating

  • Etherpad during workshop: online collaborative note-taking & chat
  • Intro to GitHub uses partners to model fork/pull cycle
  • Other coding exercises (eg code review) also use partners/groups

    • Assessment

  • Pre-workshop assessment via online form: drives workshop planning
  • Challenges during workshops provide formative assessment
  • Post-workshop assessment: still a challenge; work in progress
